Sources

The thinkers, the myth, and the science behind the Spark.

Alan Watts

Primary source for the metaphysical frame and the Narcissus image.

Watts gives the show its philosophical bones — the symbol is not the thing; our names about ourselves, our ideas of ourselves, are not ourselves; reality itself can't be named because the moment you name it, you have a model and not the thing.

The descent script in Phase 1 borrows directly from his Art of Meditation — *"close your eyes, allow your ears to hear all sounds around you. Don't try to name or identify the sounds, just hear them as you would listen to music."*

Terence McKenna

Political complement to Watts. The wound named more sharply.

Where Watts opens the cage, McKenna names who built it. His thesis: we have been kept from the wellspring of experience by dominator culture, by institutions, by ideology — and the way out is by trusting your own intuition, claiming your authenticity, acting from that without regret.

It's done through just walking away from all of that. Claiming your identity. Claiming your vision, your being, your intuition. And then acting from that without regret. Cleanly. Without regret.

Used in: closing cadence template for Phase 6 / Phase 7. Plus the optional inside aside in Phase 2 ("the gnomes have been with us all along") is a McKenna line.

Narcissus

Greek myth — Ovid, Metamorphoses Book III.

The story usually gets misread as "he loved himself too much." The reading the show takes — and the one that actually matters — is sharper:

He was not in love with himself. He was in love with an unreachable ideal-image.

That's the model trap, named perfectly by a 2,000-year-old myth. The polished version that he could see but never touch. The longing for a self he could never become. Meanwhile, the actual person standing there was invisible — even to him.

Available story extensions

Three elements of the myth not currently in the show but kept in the reference doc, available if needed:

  • Echo — the counter-figure. Where Narcissus is trapped by his own ideal-image, Echo dissolves by always reflecting others. She has no original voice. Both traps. The audience contains both.
  • The narcissus flower — grew on the spot where he died. Could function as a closing image or take-home object if the show ever wants a tangible memento.
  • Tiresias's prophecy — when Narcissus is born, the seer is asked if the boy will live a long life. He answers: "yes, if he never knows himself." The prophecy is fulfilled when Narcissus does recognise himself and dies. Reading: self-knowledge is dangerous when the self being known is just an image.

Nikola Tesla

Patron of the Spark.

Tesla isn't just the name on the device. He's the example of what the show is teaching, lived. He was a visual thinker who built inventions in his head before building them in the world — running them in his imagination so vividly he could watch them work, watch them fail, refine them, and only then make them real. He worked with what couldn't be seen. Currents. Fields. Resonance. He turned what was happening underneath everything into something you could feel.

The way the world runs on electricity tonight — every plug, every cable, every motor — is downstream of his AC system and his induction motor. He won the technical battles and lost the commercial ones again and again.

He never got to build the thing he wanted most: free wireless power for everyone. The world wasn't ready. The Spark, briefly, fulfils that dream in miniature.

The science behind the Spark

What the Tesla coil actually does. The physics of why "you're seeing wireless energy" isn't a metaphor.

What's normally happening

Air is mostly nitrogen (N₂) and oxygen (O₂). Each atom has a positive nucleus surrounded by a negative electron cloud. Normally these balance — atom is electrically neutral.

What the coil does

It generates very high-voltage AC at high frequency. The voltage creates an enormously strong electric field around the discharge point. The field is so strong it physically rips electrons off the air molecules.

What you get

ParticleChargeWhat it is
Positive ion+The atom left behind after losing an electron
Free electron−The electron that got pulled off, now flying around on its own
Photon (light)—Released when an electron drops back into an atom — the energy comes out as light

So what is "plasma"?

The soup of all the above mixed together — positive ions + free electrons + still-neutral atoms. The fourth state of matter. It conducts electricity (because the charge carriers are free) and it glows (because electrons keep recombining and emitting light).

The colour of the glow tells you what gas: nitrogen gives the purple/violet. Air is ~78% nitrogen, so a Tesla coil discharge in air is purple.

Why it crackles

The plasma channel heats super-fast → air expands → tiny shockwaves → that's the crackling sound. Same physics as thunder, just smaller.

"Wireless energy" — literally true

The coil generates an electromagnetic field that extends into the air around it. Where that field is strong enough, it ionises the air → visible plasma channel. That plasma is literally electrical energy moving through the air with no wire connecting it to anything. The show isn't being metaphorical when it says you're seeing wireless energy. You literally are.

Resonance — what's actually happening at the pairing moment

When two systems can vibrate at the same frequency, energy can transfer between them very efficiently. Push a child on a swing at the right rhythm and they go higher and higher with very little effort. Push at the wrong rhythm and you fight them.

Tesla used resonance to step voltage up dramatically (the Tesla coil is a resonant transformer), to detect very weak signals (the basis of radio), and to attempt power transmission across distance.

The psychological anchor in Phase 8 is classical conditioning — pairing a stimulus with a felt state. But "conditioning" sounds clinical. Resonance describes the same phenomenon in a way that's true to the physics happening in the room (the coil is doing literal resonance), true to the felt experience (state and word tuning to each other), and consistent with how Tesla himself thought about his work.

Spanning

In Dutch, the word for voltage is spanning — which is the same word for tension. From the verb spannen (to stretch, to pull taut). The conceptual mapping was borrowed from German Spannung in the 19th century.

The show's line — "in Dutch the word for voltage and the word for tension are the same word" — is straightforwardly true. There's no English equivalent that has this double-meaning so cleanly.

The quiet gift: the language quietly knows that the felt sense of being-about-to-happen is electrically the same as the physics of voltage across a gap. Anticipatory tension is, literally, a small physical version of voltage.

Things the show doesn't claim

A few places where the show lives near a line, and where we are honest with ourselves:

  • The widely-circulated "energy, frequency, vibration" quote attributed to Tesla has shaky sourcing — possibly apocryphal. The script doesn't use it.
  • The pairing of a felt state with a trigger word is real psychological conditioning. It works. But it's not the same physical phenomenon as electromagnetic resonance — only a metaphor. The show language blends the two on purpose.
  • "You are also lightning" is poetic. Humans are bioelectric — neurons fire by ion gradients, hearts beat on electrical pulses — but this is not the same as plasma discharge. The line works as metaphor; we're not making a physics claim.
  • Tesla held mystical / cosmic views modern physics doesn't endorse. The show borrows his way of seeing without endorsing his cosmology.
